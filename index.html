How to find the right person on LinkedIn

Don't message a company page, it goes nowhere. Find a real human on the events, community, people or marketing team. Click "Find a person on LinkedIn" on any card, or search these yourself.

What to type in LinkedIn search

Search the role, then on the results page click People, then filter by Current company (the company) and Locations (Israel, Tel Aviv or Herzliya).

  • Events Manager or Events Coordinator + company name
  • Community Manager or Community Lead + company name
  • Employer Brand or Employee Experience + company name
  • Social Media Manager or Content + company name
  • Talent Acquisition or Recruiter + company name (great for "is anything open?")
  • Office Manager or People & Culture + company name
  • For NGOs: Events, Fundraising, Development, or Volunteer Coordinator + org name

Quick rules

  • Aim for someone mid-level, a manager or coordinator. Junior people reply more and often refer you up.
  • Set Locations to Israel (Tel Aviv or Herzliya) so you reach the local team, not the US head office.
  • Send the connection note first (under 300 characters). Only send the follow-up once they accept.
  • Add one personal line where you can, mention a specific event, post or product of theirs. It doubles reply rates.
  • Keep it to one a day. Steady beats a burst that gets you flagged as spam.
  • For the Remote US/UK companies, skip the location filter and search the role plus company name worldwide. Their message is already reframed, no Israel mention, so it reads as a remote-job approach.

The honest truth

Sending 200 cold messages, on its own, gets you a few nice chats. Not a job. What turns this list into offers is making your real experience visible, a concrete move date, and applying while you network. And you have real experience: a year in marketing at a digital agency, a new event planning job, and the Amora brand you built yourself. You're not breaking in from zero, you're a marketing professional moving into events. This playbook makes that case for you. Work down it.

1

Fix your positioning

Never say "aspiring" again. You have a CV. Lead with it. Use this on your LinkedIn headline and in messages:

Marketing & events professional. 1 year at a digital agency, now planning events, and founder of Amora (perfume brand). Relocating to Israel in 2027.
  • Headline = what you ARE: "Marketing & events professional", not "looking for opportunities".
  • Lead with the year of marketing and the events job. That's the experience employers screen for.
  • Put the 2027 move in your headline and About. A date makes you hireable; "planning to move" does not.
  • About section, 3 short lines: marketing background, events now + Amora, what you're looking for in Israel.
2

Build a one-page portfolio (this week)

One link you drop into every conversation. It does the convincing so you don't have to. Easiest build: a free Notion page, a Canva site, or I can build you one like this tool.

  • Marketing - 2 lines on your year at Top Click Media and any campaigns, clients or results you can share. This is your professional anchor.
  • Events - your new event planning job, plus a photo and 2 lines on each event as you run them.
  • Amora - screenshots of the brand, site, packaging, playlists. "Built the brand, site, visual identity and content from scratch."
  • Content - 3 to 5 of your best social posts or a sample campaign.
  • Keep it to ONE page. Link it in your LinkedIn "Featured" section and in every follow-up message.
3

Turn your event planning job into visible proof

This job is your single biggest asset for the roles you want. Don't just do it, make it visible. (Stay within what your employer's happy for you to share.)

  • For each event you plan, capture a photo and note what you handled: budget, vendors, logistics, guest experience.
  • Write a short LinkedIn post when you can: what you planned, what went well, what you learned.
  • Add the role to your LinkedIn experience now, with a real description of what you do day to day.
  • This is your answer to "do you have events experience?" - "Yes, it's my job. Here's what I've run."
4

Post weekly so contacts see you're active

When someone you messaged checks your profile, they should see a person who's doing the work. Silent profiles get ignored.

  • One post a week. Behind-the-scenes of Amora, an event you ran, something you learned about brand/content/events.
  • Comment genuinely on the posts of people you're about to message, a week before you connect. Warm contacts reply, cold ones don't.
  • You don't need to go viral. You need to look alive and on-topic.
5

Network AND apply, in parallel

This tool is for the hidden job market, warm intros and being remembered. It does not replace applying.

  • Keep doing one company a day here for the relationships.
  • Also apply to live openings weekly: Secret Tel Aviv Jobs, LinkedIn, Janglo. The remote US/UK roles you can apply to from anywhere right now.
  • When you apply somewhere on your list, check if you already have a contact there, and message them too. A warm application beats a cold one every time.
6

Your next 90 days

  • Weeks 1-2: Fix LinkedIn headline + About. Add the events job + marketing role. Build the one-page portfolio. Start daily outreach here.
  • Weeks 3-6: Document the events you plan at work, add them to the portfolio. Post weekly. Keep daily outreach going.
  • Weeks 7-12: Start applying to remote US/UK roles for real. Turn your best LinkedIn chats into short video or coffee calls.
  • Throughout: nail down the move date. The moment it's firm, update everything to a specific month.

What moves the needle most

If you only do two things: build the portfolio and make your events job + marketing year visible on LinkedIn. Those turn you from "wants to break in" into "she already does this", which is the whole game. Want me to build the portfolio page next? Just ask.

This is a plan, not a guarantee. The work is yours, but you're starting from a stronger place than you think.
